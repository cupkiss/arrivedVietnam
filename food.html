Fill Your Stomach

From the North to the South of Vietnam, you'll find that tastes and cuisines vary. In the capital, locals may eat bánh xèo, which are crispy pancakes rolled in greens and dipped in sweet-and-sour fish sauce, while Saigonese eat bún chả, which is pork belly. Bún bò Huế is a popular breakfast dish in the former Imperial City, while toothsome cao lu noodles are the best quick snack in Hoi An. It would take a lifetime to eat your way through all of this delicious diversity, from simple home cooking to addictive street food to modern Vietnamese cuisine.

Northern Food in Vietnam

In comparison to central or southern Vietnam, the culture of the northern region is unique, and Hanoi is the origin of much of the cuisine of the region. The food in Hanoi is generally quite well-balanced, leaning more toward subtlety than spiciness or power. Black pepper, not chili pepper, is used to add heat to food. Northern Vietnam excels at elegance and tradition, while Southern Vietnam excels at sweet and spicy flavor profiles and fusion cuisine. The dishes are light and the result of centuries of practice.

Central Food in Vietnam

Hue is a city with a long history, one of grandeur and color. Hue was once the residence of the Nguyen Dynasty's royal families. It is in the center of Viet Nam and is only five miles from the coast. Inferable from its magnificent foundation, the food of Shade is frequently alluded to as "Imperial Cooking." Literally, the phrase "a meal fit for a king" applies. And it all began with the spellbindingly named: Prohibited Purple City. The fortified Forbidden Purple City was where the emperor, his family, and all of the people lived. The emperor, who ruled the throne, invited the best Viet Nam chefs to come and make the most stunning dishes they could conjure. These chefs created dishes that were not only delicious to the palate but also stunning to the eye, making use of ingredients and recipes from the area as well as their own regional flavors. As a result, Hue's cuisine gained a reputation for its refined aesthetics.

Southern Food in Vietnam

Saigon (Ho Chi Minh City) is truly a "hot pot" food city, avidly combining ingredients and recipes from many different regions and cultures, whereas Hanoi food is typically more delicate, sticking to traditional recipes that go lighter on seasonings and spices. Even though Saigonese cuisine has spread throughout the nation, the best is still what originated here. The wide variety of local cuisine in Vietnam's largest city, which ranges from basic street food to fine dining experiences, is overwhelming.
